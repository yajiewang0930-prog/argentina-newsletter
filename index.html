Macro & Policy

Key Developments

Argentina's economy shows a fragile recovery with sharp sectoral divergence: industrial production rebounded in March (+0.7% YoY) after eight months of decline, but the first quarter still closed negative. Inflation remains stubbornly high, with April expected to print below 3% for the first time in ten months, though household income continues to erode—90% of families have less than $60,000 daily after fixed costs. The BCRA bought over USD 7 billion in the first four months, driving the peso's best nominal appreciation in decades (+4.9%), but analysts warn this "party" has an expiration date. On the policy front, the Mercosur-EU trade agreement entered into force, and Minister Sturzenegger announced a wave of deregulation bills including real estate and AI-governed companies.

Top Stories

  • Mercosur-EU Trade Agreement Takes Effect, Unlocking Tariff Elimination on 90% of Trade: The landmark pact between the European Union and Mercosur entered into force on May 1, covering 30% of global GDP and 700 million consumers. Economy Minister Luis Caputo highlighted the agreement will boost Argentine exports, attract foreign investment, and strengthen key sectors like agribusiness and energy. The deal eliminates tariffs on 90% of bilateral trade over a transition period, with immediate reductions for Argentine beef, grains, and biofuels. For Europe, it opens access to Mercosur's automotive, machinery, and pharmaceutical markets. Analysts estimate the agreement could add 0.5% to Argentina's GDP annually once fully implemented, though local industry groups warn about increased competition from European imports. [Ambito, LA NACION]
  • Peso Enjoys Best Four-Month Streak in Decades, But Analysts Warn of Imminent Reversal: The Argentine peso appreciated 4.9% nominally in the first four months of 2026, its strongest performance in decades, driven by an extraordinary USD surplus from record harvests, Patagonian oil exports, and corporate debt issuance. The BCRA purchased over USD 7 billion during this period, fueling a lucrative carry trade that has investors celebrating. However, with inflation accumulating 9.4% in Q1, the real exchange rate is appreciating rapidly, squeezing export competitiveness. Domingo Cavallo warned that without a monetary reform and lifting of capital controls, disinflation could "bog down." Analysts at Bloomberg note that despite government incentives, most Argentines still hoard dollars outside the formal system due to lingering distrust from the 2001 corralito. [Ambito, Bloomberg]
  • Sturzenegger Unveils Six New Deregulation Bills, Including Real Estate Reform and AI-Managed Companies: Minister of Deregulation Federico Sturzenegger announced a legislative package to be sent to Congress in June, including a landmark real estate deregulation bill aimed at reducing transaction costs. Speaking before developers, he criticized the current role of real estate brokers and proposed eliminating mandatory intermediary requirements. Another bill would legally allow companies to be managed 100% by artificial intelligence, a global first. Additional measures include simplifying the Simplified Profits Tax (which critics call a "disguised amnesty"), new tools for importers to verify merchandise origin before shipping, and a "Kit 4.0" program to subsidize SME digital transformation. The package signals the government's push to maintain legislative momentum after Chief of Staff Adorni's congressional appearance was deemed a "turning point" by Casa Rosada officials. [LA NACION, Ambito]
  • Industrial Production Rebounds in March After Eight-Month Decline, But Q1 Still Negative: The Orlando Ferreres Industrial Production Index (IPI) rose 0.8% month-on-month (seasonally adjusted) and 0.7% year-on-year in March, breaking a streak of eight consecutive monthly declines. The recovery was driven by transable goods and energy sectors, with soybean crushing jumping 32.7% and oil refining expanding. However, the first quarter still closed with a 0.8% annual contraction in real investment, and the auto parts sector lost 7.7% of its workforce (over 4,000 jobs) in the past year. A survey of SMEs shows nearly 50% plan to reduce headcount in coming months. The construction sector continues to struggle despite new paritaria agreements, while commerce and services show mixed signals. [Ambito]
  • Inflation Expected to Slow Below 3% in April for First Time in Ten Months: After ten consecutive months of accelerating inflation, April's Consumer Price Index is projected to print between 2.5% and 2.9%, breaking below the key 3% threshold. This would mark the first deceleration since June 2025. However, core inflation remains sticky, and regulated prices continue to exert upward pressure—fuel alone has a carryover effect exceeding 10% despite a government freeze on gasoline prices. The BCRA acknowledged inflationary pressures but expressed confidence in a downward trend over coming months. Meanwhile, household inflation expectations for the next 30 days averaged 3.93%, suggesting consumers remain skeptical. May brings a new wave of administered price increases, including AySA water bills capped at monthly increments. [Ambito]
  • Household Financial Strain Deepens: 90% of Families Have Less Than $60,000 Daily After Fixed Costs: A new study reveals that disposable household income has collapsed from 53% of total income in 2015 to just 36% in February 2026, meaning 90% of Argentine families have less than $60,000 (approximately USD 50 at official rate) remaining daily after covering housing, utilities, transport, education, and healthcare. The UBA's RA Center report shows consumer credit surged 57% since Milei took office, with credit card usage increasingly funding basic purchases. Household delinquency rates have already surpassed pandemic-era levels, though BCRA President Santiago Bausili claims banks "have already seen the peak of non-performing loans." The government announced a 1.8% cumulative wage increase for domestic workers and a July revision, while most paritaria agreements continue to lag behind inflation. [Ambito, UBA RA Center]

Financial Markets

Key Developments

Argentina’s financial markets entered May with a mixed tone as the government successfully managed local debt rollovers while investors increasingly priced in political uncertainty tied to the 2027 electoral calendar. The blue-chip swap (CCL) and MEP dollar rates remained relatively stable, but the Treasury faces $6.9 trillion in peso-denominated maturities this month. Meanwhile, a spike in non-performing loans in Greater Buenos Aires—quadrupling over the past year—has sparked concern, though the BCRA and major banks insist the worst is behind them. On the regulatory front, new BCRA rules for digital payment fintechs aim to close legal gray areas, signaling tighter oversight of the rapidly growing sector.

Top Stories

  • Dollar Rates Hold Steady as Blue Chip and MEP Markets Remain Calm: The official dollar opened the week at ARS 1,045 for buying and ARS 1,085 for selling, while the blue dollar traded around ARS 1,230, reflecting a spread of roughly 13% against the official rate. The MEP dollar hovered near ARS 1,190 and the CCL around ARS 1,210, showing no major volatility. The euro official rate stood at ARS 1,135 and the euro blue at ARS 1,280, while the Brazilian real blue traded at ARS 195. The stability in parallel markets suggests the government’s tight monetary and fiscal controls are holding, though the lack of a clear exit strategy from capital controls keeps the premium elevated. [Ambito]
  • Treasury Faces $6.9 Trillion in Peso Maturities in May, Targets Rollover Above 100%: The Economy Ministry will confront $6.9 trillion in local-currency debt payments this month, continuing its strategy of extending maturities and achieving rollover rates above 100%. Hard-dollar placements have already surpassed $2.25 billion, signaling sustained demand for dollar-linked instruments. Investors are demanding higher yields on 2028 bonds, reflecting growing caution about the government’s ability to sustain its fiscal path amid a deteriorating economy. The Treasury’s ability to refinance without market disruption will be a key test of confidence in the coming weeks. [Ambito]
  • Non-Performing Loans Quadruple in Greater Buenos Aires, Banks Say Peak Has Passed: A BCRA report revealed that non-performing loans (NPLs) in the Greater Buenos Aires area have quadrupled over the past year, reaching levels not seen since the 2020 pandemic. The deterioration is concentrated in consumer and SME portfolios, driven by declining real wages and a sharp contraction in economic activity. Despite the alarming headline, major banks and BCRA officials argue that the NPL peak has already passed, citing early signs of stabilization in payment behavior. However, the data underscores the fragility of household finances and the risk of further credit quality erosion if the recession deepens. [Ambito]
  • BCRA Tightens Rules on Digital Payment Fintechs, Closing Legal Gray Areas: The Central Bank issued new regulations requiring fintechs operating as digital payment service providers to comply with stricter capital, liquidity, and operational requirements. The norm eliminates previous legal ambiguities, bringing these entities under the same supervisory framework as traditional payment processors. The move aims to enhance consumer protection and financial stability as digital payments surge, but could raise compliance costs for smaller fintechs. Industry players welcomed the clarity but warned that excessive regulation could stifle innovation in a sector that has been a key driver of financial inclusion. [Ambito]
  • Election Calendar Weighs on Dollar Bonds as 2027 Campaign Looms: Argentina’s dollar-denominated sovereign bonds are showing sensitivity to the emerging 2027 electoral calendar, with investors beginning to price in political risk. The government’s ability to maintain fiscal discipline and service debt will be tested as campaign spending pressures mount. Bond prices have edged lower in recent sessions, and the yield curve is steepening as longer-dated paper demands higher compensation for political uncertainty. Analysts note that while near-term financing needs are covered, the electoral horizon introduces a new layer of risk that could cap further bond appreciation. [Bloomberg Línea]

Argentina’s external debt has hit a historic record of over USD 483 billion, with short-term maturities quadrupling, as the government executed a complex operation with the U.S. Treasury to pay USD 819 million in interest to the IMF. The administration purchased Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) from the U.S. to meet its obligations, underscoring the country’s continued reliance on external lifelines. Meanwhile, Wall Street remains cautiously optimistic about Milei’s economic program but flags doubts about 2027 sustainability, and a top official resigned after plans to return to international capital markets were shelved. The economy also posted its sharpest monthly contraction under Milei, adding pressure on the stabilization agenda.

Top Stories

  • Argentina Buys USD 819 Million in SDRs from U.S. Treasury to Pay IMF Interest: The Argentine government completed a financial operation with the U.S. Treasury, purchasing Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) equivalent to USD 819 million to cover interest payments to the International Monetary Fund (IMF). This marks the second such transaction in recent months, highlighting the country’s limited access to hard currency. The operation allows Argentina to meet its IMF obligations without depleting its own reserves, but critics argue it deepens dependency on bilateral support. The payment corresponds to interest on the USD 44 billion loan extended under the 2022 program. [Infobae, La Nación, Ambito]
  • External Debt Hits Record USD 483 Billion as Short-Term Maturities Quadruple: Argentina’s external debt has reached an all-time high of over USD 483 billion, according to official data, with short-term maturities—those due within one year—multiplying by four compared to previous levels. The increase reflects aggressive borrowing by successive administrations, with the current government under Javier Milei adding to the stock despite pledges of fiscal discipline. Analysts warn that the concentration of short-term obligations raises refinancing risks, especially given the country’s limited market access. The debt load now exceeds 100% of GDP, making it one of the highest in emerging markets. [La Política Online, NEA HOY, Enterate Noticias]
  • Former IMF Negotiator Warns Argentina’s Debt Is Less Payable Under Milei: One of the key negotiators of Argentina’s 2021 IMF agreement has stated that the country’s debt is now less sustainable under President Javier Milei than under the previous administration. The official argued that Milei’s sharp devaluation and austerity measures have increased the real burden of dollar-denominated liabilities, while economic contraction reduces the government’s capacity to service them. The warning comes as Argentina prepares for a USD 1 billion disbursement from the IMF, which is tied to continued compliance with program targets. The negotiator also noted that the government’s inability to access voluntary capital markets compounds the problem. [Perfil]
  • Wall Street Eyes Milei’s Economic Program with Enthusiasm but Doubts for 2027: International investors are closely watching Milei’s economic reforms, expressing enthusiasm for his fiscal austerity and deregulation push, but harboring doubts about sustainability beyond 2027. Key concerns include the lack of a credible medium-term growth strategy, the high cost of servicing IMF debt, and the political feasibility of maintaining cuts through the next electoral cycle. Wall Street analysts note that while bond prices have rallied on the back of tighter fiscal policy, the economy remains in recession, and a return to growth is critical for debt dynamics. The next USD 1 billion IMF disbursement is seen as a short-term relief but not a solution to structural imbalances. [Infobae]
  • Top Argentine Official Resigns After Return to International Markets Shelved: A senior Argentine economic official resigned after the government shelved plans to return to international capital markets, dealing a blow to hopes of normalizing external financing. The resignation underscores internal tensions over the pace of reforms and the lack of progress in regaining market access. Without the ability to issue new bonds, Argentina remains reliant on IMF disbursements and bilateral support to meet its obligations. The official’s departure raises questions about the coherence of Milei’s economic team and the feasibility of its long-term strategy. [Buenos Aires Times]
  • Argentina’s Economy Records Sharpest Monthly Contraction Under Milei: Argentina’s economy posted its steepest monthly contraction since Javier Milei took office, with economic activity falling 3.2% month-on-month in March. The decline was driven by sharp drops in construction, manufacturing, and retail sales, as austerity measures crushed domestic demand. The contraction underscores the severe cost of Milei’s stabilization program, which has slashed public spending and devalued the peso by over 50%. While inflation has moderated from its peak, the recession deepens social hardship and raises doubts about the political sustainability of the adjustment. [Financial Times]

The investment will be used to expand belo’s stablecoin services across Latin America, targeting markets with high inflation and limited access to traditional banking. Belo, which already operates in Argentina and Mexico, plans to use the capital to enhance its product suite, including crypto-to-fiat conversion and remittance services. This marks one of the largest venture capital rounds in the Argentine crypto space this year, highlighting Tether’s strategy to deepen its footprint in the region. The deal also reflects growing demand for dollar-pegged digital assets as a store of value in economies with volatile local currencies. [Tech Funding News]
