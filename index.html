Macro & Policy

Key Developments

Argentina’s macro landscape in this period was defined by a deepening political crisis around Chief of Staff Manuel Adorni, mixed economic signals, and significant policy moves. President Javier Milei publicly defended Adorni against corruption allegations, while internal LLA tensions surfaced. On the economic front, the government approved the 2026 consolidated budget, reglamented the labor formalization incentive (RIFL), and saw the Mercosur-EU agreement come into force. However, data showed a fragile recovery: auto production plunged 18.6% in the first four months, mining output hit a record, and provincial co-participation transfers fell 5.6% in real terms year-to-date. The BCRA bought over USD 7 billion in the first quarter, but 99% of reserve growth came from gold revaluation, not intervention.

Top Stories

  • Milei Defends Adorni Amid Corruption Probe, Internal LLA Rift Widens: President Javier Milei gave an exclusive interview to LN+ to defend his Chief of Staff, Manuel Adorni, who is under investigation for alleged illicit enrichment. Milei stated he would not "execute an innocent person" and that Adorni would advance his sworn asset declaration. This came after Senator Patricia Bullrich publicly demanded Adorni present his declaration immediately, warning that the accusations "bog down" the administration. The episode marks the first time a high-profile LLA figure has publicly diverged on such a sensitive matter, exposing tensions between Bullrich and Milei’s sister, Karina Milei, over Adorni’s role in the government. [LA NACION]
  • Government Reglamentates RIFL Labor Formalization Regime, Approves 2026 Budget: The government of Javier Milei reglamented the Régimen de Incentivo a la Formalización Laboral (RIFL) via Decree 315/2026, a key chapter of the labor reform. The regime aims to incorporate unregistered workers, the unemployed, and monotributistas by offering reduced employer contributions for new hires, with benefits extendable up to 48 months. Separately, the government approved the 2026 Consolidated Budget, which integrates the entire public sector, projects a financial surplus, and does not modify existing budgets. The RIFL is designed to boost formal employment, with conditions and limits to prevent abuse. [Ambito]
  • Mercosur-EU Agreement Enters Force; Argentina Ships First Tariff-Free Honey to Germany: The landmark Mercosur-European Union trade agreement came into effect on May 1, eliminating tariffs on 90% of trade between the two blocs, which together represent 30% of global GDP and 700 million consumers. Argentina immediately capitalized, exporting its first shipment of over 20,000 kg of honey from Concordia, Entre Ríos, to Germany under the new zero-tariff quota system. Minister of Economy Luis Caputo highlighted the agreement's potential to boost exports, drive key sectors, and attract capital. Separately, Minister Caputo announced a Chevron investment of over USD 10 billion under the RIGI (Large Investment Incentive Regime). [Ambito]
  • Economic Activity Shows Fragile Recovery in March, Auto Sector Plunges 18.6%: March data revealed a "two-speed" economy: mining output hit a record, with the IPI mining index rising 10.4% year-on-year (lithium up 70% annually, salt more than doubled), and soybean crushing jumped 32.7%. However, auto production failed to sustain its rebound, plunging 18.6% in the first four months of 2026, hit by trade opening and weaker domestic sales. Real investment fell 0.8% annually in March (vs. -11.1% in February), while IVA tax collection data confirmed consumption is still stagnant, with the domestic-market component down 2% year-on-year in the first four months. [Ambito]
  • Provincial Finances Under Severe Strain as Co-participation Transfers Fall 5.6% in Real Terms: Provinces lost ARS 1.3 trillion in co-participation resources in the first four months of 2026. While total tax revenue exceeded ARS 68 trillion, only 32% was shared with provinces. In real terms, automatic transfers fell 5.6% year-on-year. Non-automatic transfers in April dropped 53% compared to the same month last year, with ATN (Treasury Contributions) to nine provinces concentrating 30% of the total. This fiscal squeeze is pressuring provincial budgets and increasing tensions with the national government. [Ambito]
  • BCRA Buys USD 7 Billion in Q1, but 99% of Reserve Growth Comes from Gold Revaluation: The Central Bank purchased over USD 7 billion in the first quarter of 2026, but a private report revealed that 99% of the increase in international reserves was due to the revaluation of gold assets. The net balance from FX intervention was practically zero. This underscores the fragility of the reserve accumulation, as it is not driven by genuine dollar purchases but by asset price fluctuations. Meanwhile, Bloomberg warned that despite government incentives, most Argentines continue to hold dollars outside the formal system, with the memory of the corralito and persistent lack of trust conditioning behavior. [Ambito, Perfil]
  • Inflation Slows in April, but Over Half of Families Cannot Afford Basic Food Basket: Inflation decelerated in April, but a study by IETSE found that more than 50% of families could not cover the basic food basket. Among those who could, 71.4% required state assistance. The population's expected inflation for April was 3.93% on average. Meanwhile, the government approved gas tariff increases with a 25% subsidy for users, and bus fares in Buenos Aires province rose 11.6% in May. The peso experienced its best four-month period in decades, appreciating 4.9% nominally while inflation accumulated 9.4% in Q1, driven by a record harvest, Patagonian oil, and corporate debt. [Ambito]

Financial Markets

Key Developments

Argentine financial markets experienced a significant rally this week, driven by a sovereign credit rating upgrade from Fitch. The blue-chip swap (CCL) and MEP dollar rates narrowed, while the blue dollar retreated to near one-month lows. The Fondo de Garantía de Sustentabilidad (FGS) of ANSES increased its equity stakes in key companies, and global markets surged on optimism over a potential US-Iran peace deal.

Top Stories

  • Sovereign Bonds and Equities Surge After Fitch Upgrade: Argentine assets soared after Fitch Ratings upgraded the country's sovereign debt. ADRs listed on Wall Street jumped up to 11%, led by banking stocks, while the country risk index plummeted 7%. Sovereign dollar bonds, particularly long-dated Globales, rebounded strongly. The upgrade opens the door for improved financing conditions and potential re-entry into international capital markets, signaling growing investor confidence in Argentina's fiscal trajectory. [Ambito - Finanzas]
  • Blue Dollar Retreats to Near One-Month Low, Official Rate Weakens: The parallel dollar fell by $15 to trade near its lowest level in almost a month, reducing the gap with the official exchange rate to under 1%. The official dollar also weakened, hitting a two-week low, which widened its spread with the crawling peg band. This gives the Central Bank more room to intervene in the official foreign exchange market (MLC) without needing to sell reserves, as it can buy dollars at a more favorable rate. [Ambito - Finanzas]
  • ANSES's FGS Boosts Equity Holdings in Key Sectors: The Fondo de Garantía de Sustentabilidad (FGS) of ANSES has increased its stake in several major Argentine companies, particularly in the energy and financial services sectors. According to data reconstructed by Ámbito up to April 2025, this increased participation has allowed the fund to appoint directors in shareholder meetings. The move signals a more active state role in corporate governance and could influence strategic decisions in these industries. [Ambito - Finanzas]
  • Wall Street Hits Records on US-Iran Peace Hopes, Oil Retreats: Major US stock indices broke new records amid expectations that the White House is close to signing a memorandum to end the war with Iran. This optimism drove a global rally in equities. Conversely, crude oil prices retreated to near $100 per barrel, as the prospect of de-escalation in the Middle East eased supply concerns. The decline in oil prices weighed on Argentine energy ADRs, which underperformed the broader rally. [Ambito - Finanzas]
  • Bitcoin Hits 3-Month High on Geopolitical and Regulatory Hopes: Bitcoin recorded its sixth gain in seven sessions, touching a new high not seen in over three months. The rally is supported by growing expectations for an end to the Middle East conflict and key developments in the US: the progress of the "Clarity Act" for crypto regulation and anticipation of the Friday employment data. The bipartisan agreement on a stablecoin bill in the Senate is also boosting sentiment, though tensions between banks and crypto exchanges persist. [Ambito - Finanzas]

International Impact

Key Developments

Argentina’s external debt has reached a record high of over USD 483 billion, with short-term maturities rising sharply, even as the Milei administration secured a USD 1 billion disbursement from the IMF. In a notable shift, a U.S. credit rating agency upgraded Argentina’s sovereign debt rating, while the government executed a USD 819 million purchase of Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) from the United States to meet IMF interest payments. However, a former IMF negotiator warned that the debt is now less payable under Milei, and the Financial Times reports the president is “battered by scandals and a slowing economy,” highlighting a tense external financing outlook.

Top Stories

  • U.S. Credit Rating Agency Upgrades Argentina’s Sovereign Debt: A major U.S. credit rating agency has upgraded Argentina’s sovereign debt rating, marking a rare positive signal for the country’s international standing. The upgrade reflects recent fiscal consolidation efforts under President Milei, including a primary fiscal surplus and tighter monetary policy. However, the rating remains deep in speculative-grade territory, and analysts caution that the upgrade is contingent on sustained policy execution. The move could lower borrowing costs for Argentina in international markets, but access remains limited given the country’s default history and high debt-to-GDP ratio. [Infobae]
  • Argentina Buys USD 819 Million in SDRs from U.S. to Pay IMF Interest: The Argentine government purchased Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) worth USD 819 million from the United States to cover interest payments to the International Monetary Fund, according to La Nación. This marks the second such transaction in recent months, following a similar USD 800 million purchase reported by Ámbito. The maneuver allows Argentina to meet its IMF obligations without drawing down its own depleted foreign reserves. However, it underscores the country’s continued reliance on bilateral support to service its debt, as the Central Bank’s net reserves remain negative. The SDR purchases are part of a broader strategy to avoid default while negotiations for a new IMF program continue. [La Nación / Ámbito]
  • Argentina’s External Debt Hits Record USD 483 Billion, Short-Term Maturities Surge: Argentina’s total external debt has reached an all-time high of USD 483 billion, according to a report from NEA HOY, with short-term maturities growing rapidly. The report highlights that the debt burden has increased under successive administrations, with the current government adding to the stock despite pledges of fiscal discipline. The surge in short-term obligations raises refinancing risks, as Argentina must roll over a larger portion of debt in a high-interest-rate environment. The government argues that the debt is sustainable, but critics point to the growing interest burden, which consumes an increasing share of fiscal revenue. [NEA HOY / Infobae]
  • IMF Disburses USD 1 Billion to Argentina, Bolstering Central Bank Reserves: The International Monetary Fund approved a new disbursement of USD 1 billion to Argentina, providing a temporary boost to the Central Bank’s foreign reserves. The funds are part of the existing Extended Fund Facility (EFF) program, which remains under review. The disbursement comes as Argentina faces a tight external payments schedule, with USD 3.2 billion in IMF principal payments due in the second half of 2026. While the injection helps stabilize reserves in the near term, it does not address the structural imbalance in Argentina’s balance of payments. The government is expected to seek a new program with the IMF later this year. [MisionesOnline]
  • Former IMF Negotiator Warns Argentina’s Debt Is Less Payable Under Milei: A former negotiator of Argentina’s 2021 IMF deal has warned that the country’s debt is now less payable under President Milei than under the previous administration. In an interview with Perfil, the ex-official argued that Milei’s aggressive austerity measures have depressed economic activity, reducing the tax base and making it harder to generate the fiscal surpluses needed to service debt. The warning comes as Argentina’s economy contracts, with GDP expected to fall 3.5% in 2026. The government disputes this view, pointing to a primary fiscal surplus of 0.5% of GDP in Q1 2026. [Perfil]
  • Financial Times: Milei Battered by Scandals and Slowing Economy: The Financial Times reports that President Javier Milei is facing mounting political and economic headwinds, including a series of corruption scandals involving close aides and a sharper-than-expected economic slowdown. The article notes that Milei’s approval ratings have fallen below 40% for the first time, as voters grow impatient with austerity. The slowdown is complicating debt negotiations with the IMF, as the fund demands deeper spending cuts that could further depress growth. The FT concludes that Milei’s reform agenda is at risk unless he can regain political momentum and stabilize the economy. [Financial Times]

Top Stories

  • Tether Leads $14M Series A in Argentine Crypto Fintech belo to Scale Stablecoins Across Latin America: Tether, the issuer of the world’s largest stablecoin (USDT), has led a $14 million Series A funding round in Argentine fintech belo, marking one of the largest crypto-focused investments in the region this year. The capital will be used to scale belo’s stablecoin infrastructure across Latin America, targeting markets with high inflation and limited access to USD. Belo currently offers a crypto wallet and payment platform that allows users to buy, sell, and transfer stablecoins, with a focus on remittances and savings. The investment signals Tether’s strategic push into Latin America, where demand for dollar-pegged digital assets is surging as local currencies depreciate. For Argentina, this deal reinforces the country’s role as a fintech innovation hub despite ongoing economic instability. [Tech Funding News]
