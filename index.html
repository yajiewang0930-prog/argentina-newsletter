Macro & Policy

Key Developments

Argentina’s political landscape is fracturing as the PRO party publicly breaks with President Milei, while internal Cabinet tensions over Chief of Staff Adorni’s scandal deepen. On the economic front, the BCRA has accumulated over US$7 billion in reserves during the first four months of the year, yet the real economy shows troubling signs: SME retail sales fell 3.2% in April, used car sales dropped 2.6%, and analysts warn that high nominal credit rates (near 70%) are choking consumption despite a projected 24% inflation. The government faces a critical test in June when labor reform subsidies (FAL) costing 0.37% of GDP must begin, potentially jeopardizing the fiscal surplus target of 1.4%.

Top Stories

  • PRO Party Breaks with Milei as Cabinet Crisis Deepens Over Adorni Scandal: Mauricio Macri’s PRO party issued a harsh communiqué distancing itself from the government, stating that the administration “demands sacrifices it is not willing to make” and that supporting the government “does not mean applauding everything.” The rupture comes amid a deepening crisis over Chief of Staff Guillermo Adorni, who is under investigation for alleged irregularities in his sworn asset declarations. President Milei has publicly backed Adorni, but Interior Minister Patricia Bullrich maintained a dissident stance, revealing growing internal fractures. Economy Minister Luis Caputo confirmed he discussed the matter with Milei and expressed confidence in the President’s support. The political instability is raising concerns in financial markets about the government’s ability to push through its reform agenda. [LA NACION]
  • Economy Shows Divergent Signals: Export Boom vs. Collapsing Domestic Consumption: While the BCRA purchased over US$7 billion in the first four months of 2026 and Fitch upgraded Argentina’s sovereign debt rating, the real economy is stagnating. SME retail sales fell 3.2% year-on-year in April, with six of seven sectors declining, according to CAME. Used car sales dropped 2.6% to 155,000 units, with the sector blaming insufficient financing rates. Analysts describe a “vacuum cleaner effect” where the export boom improves external accounts but fails to trickle down to consumers, as real wages continue to erode. Marina Dal Poggetto of EcoGo warned that the cost of living in dollar terms is rising, while the industrial production index (IPI) showed a modest positive reading that masks underlying weakness. [Ambito]
  • High Credit Rates and Low Deposit Yields Create a Financial Trap for Households: With inflation projected at 24% but lending rates near 70%, the financial system is creating a severe drag on economic recovery. Economists Miguel Kiguel, Martín Redrado, and Federico González Rouco agree that the key obstacle to mortgage credit—and broader economic reactivation—is not just macroeconomic stability but the structural mismatch between 20-30 year loans and 30-day deposits. For savers, the situation is equally grim: a $1 million fixed-term deposit now yields only about $8,000 per month, far below inflation. To generate a $50,000 monthly profit, investors would need to deposit over $6 million, a threshold that excludes most households. This “nominality trap” is discouraging consumption, increasing delinquency, and exposing deep inefficiencies in the banking system. [Ambito]
  • Molinos Agro Proposes $800 Million Mega-Industrial Complex in Santa Fe: Molinos Agro presented a preliminary project to invest US$800 million in Timbúes, Santa Fe, to build what it calls the “largest-scale complex in the world.” The facility would include three oilseed processing and crushing lines, each with a daily capacity of 12,000 to 15,000 tons, storage for approximately one million tons of grains and oilseeds, and two docks. This investment signals strong confidence in Argentina’s agricultural export potential, particularly as the country benefits from a global commodity supercycle. However, the project’s viability may depend on political stability and the government’s ability to maintain competitive export taxes and labor conditions. [LA NACION]
  • BCRA Acknowledges Inflationary Pressure but Forecasts Easing in Coming Months: The Central Bank admitted to ongoing inflationary pressures in its latest monetary policy report but expressed confidence that inflation will decelerate in the coming months. This comes as the price of beef—which had been rising 10% monthly—stabilized in April with no variation, though it remains the most expensive protein. The BCRA’s reserve accumulation of over US$7 billion in the first four months provides some buffer, but analysts warn that the real test will come in June when the government must begin implementing labor reform subsidies (FAL) costing 0.37% of GDP, which could pressure the fiscal surplus target of 1.4% if tax revenues underperform. [Perfil / Diario Mendoza]

Financial Markets

Key Developments

Argentina’s financial markets experienced a significant rally mid-week after Fitch Ratings upgraded the country’s sovereign credit rating, sending local bonds soaring and the risk premium plunging to a three-month low. The BCRA also posted its largest daily FX purchase of the month, pushing gross reserves back above $46 billion. However, the rally was tempered by a sharp sell-off in Argentine ADRs on Friday, led by a 12% drop in Mercado Libre, while the blue-chip dollar remained flat for a third consecutive session. Meanwhile, household loan delinquency hit an alarming 11.5% in March, the highest since 2004, signaling growing financial strain on consumers.

Argentine ADRs on Wall Street surged up to 11% on the day, while sovereign bonds posted firm gains, pushing the country risk index down 7% to its lowest level in three months. The upgrade, which Moody’s hinted could be followed by a similar move, also fueled speculation about Argentina’s potential return to emerging market status when MSCI reviews its classification in June. Gross international reserves rose by $105 million to $46.056 billion, providing a modest buffer ahead of upcoming sovereign debt payments. The intervention helped stabilize the official exchange rate, which remains tightly managed, while the blue-chip dollar held flat at around $1,200, now trading $20 below the retail bank rate for the second consecutive week. Among the 30 largest financial institutions, which account for 96% of total credit, 24 reported an increase in delinquency rates during the month. The deterioration reflects the ongoing squeeze on real incomes amid high inflation and a sluggish economic recovery. The government is reportedly exploring measures to contain the problem, including potential debt restructuring programs for vulnerable borrowers, though details remain scarce. The rising default rate poses a growing risk to bank balance sheets and consumer lending growth. [Ámbito]

The changes include reductions to the Judicial Council’s budget and a redistribution of positions across ministries. The cuts are part of the administration’s ongoing fiscal tightening efforts to meet the primary surplus target agreed with the IMF. The budget revision comes as inflation remains elevated — the next INDEC print is expected to show monthly CPI around 4-5% — and as the government seeks to reassure markets of its commitment to fiscal discipline ahead of mid-year reviews.   • New Batch of 13 CEDEARs Launches, Tapping AI, Energy, and Rare Earths Themes: The Buenos Aires stock exchange listed 13 new CEDEARs (Argentine depositary receipts) covering foreign companies in high-growth sectors including artificial intelligence, cybersecurity, data centers, energy (oil, gas, uranium), rare earths, real estate, and digital payments. The launch expands local investors’ access to global thematic plays without needing a foreign broker. Separately, three existing CEDEARs — all in AI, chip manufacturing, and space technology — have more than doubled in value year-to-date, reflecting strong retail demand for tech exposure. The new listings are expected to further boost trading volumes on the local bourse. [Ámbito]

  • How Much Debt Did Previous Governments Accumulate and What Is Milei's Partial Balance?: A detailed analysis by Infobae reveals the trajectory of Argentina's public debt under successive administrations, providing context for President Milei's fiscal management. The report shows that total public debt reached $420 billion by the end of 2025, with the current administration reducing the debt-to-GDP ratio from 89% to 82% through aggressive spending cuts and debt buybacks. However, the government has added $15 billion in new debt to the IMF and bilateral creditors. The analysis highlights that the Kirchner administrations (2003-2015) increased debt by $180 billion, while Macri's government added $100 billion, and Fernandez's term saw a $90 billion increase.   • Argentina's Resource Wealth and Economic Structure: A Britannica Analysis: Britannica's comprehensive profile of Argentina's economy underscores the country's significant natural resource advantages that continue to attract international investment despite political instability. Argentina holds the world's second-largest shale gas reserves (Vaca Muerta) and is the third-largest lithium producer globally, with lithium exports growing 45% year-over-year to $2.8 billion in 2025. The agricultural sector, particularly soybeans and corn, contributes 7% of GDP and accounts for 35% of total exports. However, the analysis notes that manufacturing has declined to 15% of GDP from 20% a decade ago, while the services sector now represents 65% of economic output. The country's infrastructure deficit, particularly in transportation and energy transmission, remains a key constraint on growth, requiring an estimated $50 billion in investment over the next decade.
