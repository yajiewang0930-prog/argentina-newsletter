Dollar Surges 5% in June — Carry Trade Breaks for First Time Since Milei's Election

The Argentine peso's months-long winning streak came to an abrupt halt in June. The official exchange rate surged nearly 5% — the biggest monthly depreciation since November 2025 — while the blue dollar hit $1,515. A confluence of global and local headwinds — a strengthening US dollar (DXY +2.2%), collapsing commodity prices (oil −12% in a single week), and the seasonal wind-down of agricultural FX liquidation — forced the BCRA to pivot from aggressive reserve accumulation to defensive intervention via dollar-linked bond sales and futures contracts. For the first time since September 2025, the peso carry trade posted a monthly loss, erasing roughly 2.5–2.7% in dollar terms.

📈 Macro & Policy

June marked a turning point for Argentina's exchange rate regime. After months of placid appreciation, the peso came under significant pressure from external and seasonal forces. The BCRA successfully defended the crawling peg band but had to deploy dollar-linked bond sales and futures contracts for the first time this year. Consumer confidence posted a surprising 6.4% jump, even as equity markets sold off sharply, reflecting a complex and increasingly divergent macro picture heading into H2.

  • Peso Slides 5% in June — Biggest Monthly Drop Since November 2025

    high

    The official exchange rate surged approximately 5% in June, closing at $1,477 per dollar — the largest monthly depreciation since November 2025. The blue dollar reached $1,515, while the MEP dollar breached $1,500 and the CCL touched $1,544. The move was driven by four converging forces: a global dollar rally (DXY up 2.2%), collapsing commodity prices (Brent crude down ~12% in a single week, soybeans down ~8%), the seasonal decline in agricultural export settlements (May liquidations fell to $2.68 billion versus the $3.53 billion historical average), and pre-electoral dollarization of portfolios ahead of 2027. The peso carry trade — which had delivered consistent positive returns since Milei's election — posted its first monthly loss, eroding 2.5–2.7% in dollar terms. The BCRA's crawling peg band set the ceiling at $1,807, with the peso trading roughly 22% below it, still within the managed float corridor.

  • BCRA Pivots to Defensive Mode: Sells Dollar-Linked Bonds, Ramps Up Futures

    medium

    The BCRA abruptly shifted its intervention strategy in June as dollar demand intensified. After months of aggressive spot-market purchases — accumulating $11.04 billion year-to-date — the Central Bank saw its daily buying pace collapse from $140 million in April–May to just $48–65 million in June. To contain exchange rate volatility without burning reserves, the BCRA sold approximately $1.5 billion in dollar-linked bonds (primarily TZV26 and D31L6) and expanded its short position in ROFEX futures contracts from $200 million to roughly $900 million. Deputy Governor Vladimir Werning, speaking at the 43rd IAEF Congress on June 3, confirmed that the BCRA had fully eliminated the policy interest rate and now relies exclusively on monetary aggregate control as its nominal anchor. Gross international reserves closed June at $46.96 billion, with net reserves at $2.8 billion — a dramatic improvement from negative $940 million at end-2025.

🏦 Financial & Credit

Argentina's consumer credit crisis deepened further this cycle. Household NPLs climbed to 12.1%, with 6.3 million Argentines now in delinquency. The gap between bank and non-bank lending rates widened dramatically — fintech lenders charge up to 60 percentage points more than traditional banks. In response, Banco Nación launched an unprecedented 120-month UVA-denominated refinancing program at subsidized rates, while Banco Ciudad and Banco Provincia rolled out parallel initiatives.

  • Household NPL Ratio Hits 12.1% — 6.3 Million Argentines in Default

    high

    The BCRA reported that household credit irregularity reached 12.1% of the total portfolio in April 2026, up from 11.5% in March and triple the 3.3% recorded a year earlier. Total system-wide NPLs across all segments reached 7.3%. According to Banco Provincia estimates, 6.3 million people are now affected. The deterioration is driven by continued real wage erosion — disposable income remains 12% below pre-pandemic levels — compounded by a cycle of serial refinancing in which households take on new high-interest debt to service existing obligations. A Fundación Éforo study found that personal debt per borrower tripled between December 2023 and January 2026, with the average surpassing ARS 1 million. Category 3, 4, and 5 borrowers — representing medium, high, and unrecoverable risk — now constitute a material share of banks' consumer books.

  • Banco Nación Launches 120-Month UVA Refinancing for Delinquent Borrowers

    high

    Banco Nación launched a sweeping Program for the Regularization of Clients in Arrears, effective June 29. The centerpiece is a UVA-denominated credit line offering up to 120-month (10-year) repayment at a subsidized fixed rate of 12% TNA for payroll customers and 14% for others, with principal adjusted by the CER inflation index. The program targets BCRA Category 3, 4, or 5 borrowers — medium to unrecoverable delinquency — and includes an optional cap tied to the Salary Variation Coefficient (CVS). Parallel lines include debt consolidation up to ARS 100 million at 65% TNA over 72 months for Category 1–2 borrowers, and credit card refinancing up to ARS 10 million at 35% TNA over 60 months. Banco Ciudad and Banco Provincia announced comparable programs, with the latter offering 72-month terms at 62.5–72.5% TNA.

  • BCRA Policy Rate Eliminated; Term Deposit Rates Purely Market-Driven

    medium

    Deputy Governor Vladimir Werning confirmed on June 3 that the BCRA has permanently retired the policy interest rate, completing the Phase 3 stabilization transition. Argentina now operates under a pure monetary aggregate control regime — the only G20 economy to do so. Term deposit rates are market-determined: major banks offer 17–19% TNA (Banco Nación 19%, Galicia 18.25%), while smaller institutions compete at 22–23%. The Badlar rate for deposits above ARS 1 million stands at 22.2%. With May inflation at 2.1% and June expectations around 2%, real rates remain mildly negative. The BCRA manages liquidity through repos, open market operations, and reserve requirements. The IMF has praised the framework's simplicity but warned that without a policy rate instrument, the BCRA's ability to respond to exchange rate shocks is constrained — a concern that gained urgency during June's dollar surge.

💳 Fintech

Argentina's fintech sector experienced a transformative two weeks. The BCRA approved Cocos' acquisition of Banco Voii, marking the country's first fintech-to-bank conversion via M&A. Open Finance implementation accelerated as the BCRA defined API and consent management standards under Decree 353/2025. Milei exempted registered crypto platforms from the cheque tax, and the BCRA imposed new AML/CFT self-assessment obligations on payment service providers. The sector is maturing rapidly but faces headwinds from the delinquency crisis.

  • Open Finance Framework Takes Shape: 89 Wallets, API Standards in Progress

    high

    Argentina's Sistema de Finanzas Abiertas (Open Finance System), established by Decree 353/2025, entered its implementation phase in June. The BCRA is actively defining technical standards for APIs, consent management protocols, cybersecurity requirements, and AI-driven credit scoring frameworks. With 89 interoperable digital wallets already registered, the system aims to unlock credit access for freelancers, monotributistas, SMEs, and individuals with thin credit histories — a population segment largely excluded from traditional bank lending. Industry CIOs are preparing for a regulatory shift that will require banks, fintechs, and payment processors to share consumer-permissioned data through standardized interfaces. The Cámara Argentina Fintech, led by Diego Díaz, welcomed the initiative but called for uniform tax treatment between banks and fintechs, arguing that discriminatory taxes — particularly Ingresos Brutos withholdings — undermine the level playing field that Open Finance seeks to create.

In a parallel move on June 10, the CNV expanded its tokenization regime, allowing onchain representation of securities — including shares, bonds, and fund units — for smaller companies seeking capital market access. The combined measures position Argentina among Latin America's most crypto-friendly jurisdictions, a notable shift given the country's historical capital controls.     medium

The regulation requires PSPCPs (Payment Service Providers that Offer Credit) to document their compliance frameworks, transaction monitoring systems, customer due diligence procedures, and suspicious activity reporting mechanisms. The move responds directly to the IMF's May 2026 recommendation for consolidated supervision of the rapidly growing non-bank credit sector, where delinquencies have surged past 30%. Industry compliance costs are expected to rise, particularly for smaller fintechs without established legal and compliance teams, potentially accelerating consolidation in a sector already strained by the delinquency crisis.
